model
This section consolidates the uid-mechanical assumptions and the exact pressure-drop quantities

Fluid-mechanical assumptions used by the pressure-loss and aeration-discharge model.

 Internal passages are treated as smooth circular tubes. Tube friction is represented by Darcy
Weisbach pressure loss with 64/Re in laminar ow and Churchill's explicit smooth-tube relation
otherwise, and abrupt area changes are represented by standard minor-loss coecients rather
than resolved separation or secondary-ow models.

 Flow is not treated with a full compressible momentum formulation.

The code does update

local density from the ideal-gas relation using the local temperature and pressure state, but the
friction, minor-loss, and perforation-discharge closures are all used in their incompressible-form
reduced-order versions.

 The optional upstream header loss and the unresolved internal splitter/manifold loss are represented by textbook tting-loss surrogates from the CrowlLouvar 2-K method.
 Parallel aeration branches are assumed identical at a given operating point, so one representative
closed-end perforated branch is marched axially and then scaled up.

 Perforations are smeared uniformly over the congured segment count rather than resolved hole
by hole. The end cap itself is not perforated.

9

 When an open top or top vent is present, the bed-side pressure opposing perforation discharge is
derived segmentwise from a reduced-order Ergun porous-bed escape model leading to the open
top or nearest vent. If no such ambient-side opening exists, the current code falls back to the
congured bed-side boundary gauge pressure.

 The porous-bed closure is still reduced-order: it uses one representative tube-center height and
one tributary top area per tube segment rather than a full three-dimensional porous-bed or
headspace-ow network.

 The marched tube gauge pressure is not allowed to fall below the local bed-side gauge pressure
used for that segment, so the reduced-order march does not permit reversal of the intended
discharge direction.
