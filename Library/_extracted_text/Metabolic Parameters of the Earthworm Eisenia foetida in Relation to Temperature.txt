INTRODUCTION 
Despite suggestions that Eisenia foetida (Savigny) may be useful for man- 
agement of certain organic wastes'-4 and for production of opti- 
mum temperature for total biomass production by this earthworm over a 
long period is not known. Working with isolated individuals, Tsukamoto and 
Watanabe8 showed that growth from hatchling to adult occurred more rap- 
idly at 25°C than at 2OOC. Data of Kirberger9 indicate a zone of metabolic 
thermal compensation between 15 and 20°C for fed, but not fasted E. 
foetida, provided the worms had been acclimated at 15°C and not 25°C. In 
contrast, data of Mitchell'O show that this zone lies between 20 and 25°C. 
Mitchell's finding was confirmed by Moment and Habermam" who also 
showed that this finding is independent of acclimation temperature. Rei- 
necke and Kriel12 obtained 194 cocoons in 20 days from E. foetida at 25°C 
compared to 106 cocoons at 2OoC, but only 2.5 hatchlings per cocoon at 25°C 
compared to 3.1 at 20°C; since 98% of 100 cocoons hatched at 20°C and 
94% at 25"C, a total of 322 progeny were derived at 20°C versus 456 at 25°C. 
The objectives of this study were to determine survival, onset of hatching, 
onset of sexual maturity, fecundity, growth rate, and total biomass pro- 
duction in relation to both population density and temperature, and to de- 
termine the optimum temperature at which these phenomena, collectively, 
would work to maximize biomass production over a period in excess of the in- 
terharvest period generally used by commercial growers. '

dishes which contained a single worm.

Optimum Temperature for Total Production 
Production of total biomass (cocoons and adults which survived 80 days) 
occurred optimally between 18 and 25"C, maximally so at 20°C and with a 
precipitous dropoff above 25°C (Fig. 1). 
DISCUSSION 
Sexual maturity was reached earlier at 25°C than at 20°C, (Table 11), but 
a similar number of cocoons was produced at both temperatures (Table IV). 
Development within cocoons occurred faster at 25°C than at 20°C-which 
confirms the work of Tsukamoto and Watanabe8-and although this might 
accelerate population growth at the higher temperature, significantly fewer 
hatchlings emerge at 25°C than at 20°C.8,12 Regression analyses and analysis 
of variance of growth rates and final mean weight of individuals in popula- 
tions of two, four, or eight earthworms revealed nonsignificant differences at 
18, 20, and 25OC; this agrees with results for rate and production of total 
biomass (Table -11). Difference in respiratory rate at 20 and 25°C was non- 
significant,l0*" and slope between mean rates of weight loss during starva- 
tion was significantly smaller between 20 and 25°C than at 15-20°C and 
2530°C (unpublished experiment): the metabolic zone of thermal neutrality 
is thus the same for catabolism and anabolism.

EPILOGUE 
Nearly all commercial worm growers in the United States market E. 
foetidu as their product, though some individuals may also produce the ex- 
otic African nightcrawler Eudrilus eugeniae or pheretemoids of the genus 
Amynthas. The only marketplace for earthworms presently are bait shops 
(for fishing) and seed stores (for alleged improvement of garden soils). 
Much of the basic science needed for commercial production of earth- 
worms has been referred to re~ently.~ Based on that report, it appears earth- 
worms could be used in management of manures, where failure to do so in 
areas that are frostbound during much of the winter could bring the farmer 
into conflict with environmental law; l6 use of E. foetida might in fact provide 
a potentially economic solution to this problem. 
Whether E. foetidu can be used economically for protein production, 
however, depends primarily on a) whether low yields can be shown to be pro- 
fitable, assuming a source of food is used once, and b) whether a source of 
food can be used repeatedly, thus obtaining multiple low yields which sum up 
to a profitable amount. Independently of whether the source of food contains 
0.5 or 5% N, and a corresponding tenfold range of other essential mineral 
nutrients, yield is about 5 g dry E. foetidu per initial 100 g dry food source.17 
(This ratio ignores whatever quantities of organic material disappear due to 
microbial metabolism.) This low yield of 5% is probably linked to castings 
toxicity, l8 wherein E. foetida will only consume a specific fraction of the food 
source in which it itself resides and into which it deposits its wastes. 
The low yield perhaps militates against use of E. foetida, on economic 
grounds, for protein production. On the other hand, should favorable micro- 
biological conditions exist for rapid detoxication of the spent medium 
(castings) following harvest of earthworms, and assuming a sufficient residue 
of microbial biomass and cellulose in the spent medium, a potential for 
economic production might exist. To realize this potential, hatchlings from 
the cocoons in the spent medium could be allowed to grow 8-10 weeks to ob- 
tain another 5%yield. Conceivably, this cycle of periodic harvest and growth 
upon spent medium could be repeated until microbial biomass, cellulose, or 
some other essential nutrient of E. foetida19 becomes limiting. 
All of this is in need of further research, but the possibility of rapidly con- 
verting straw into artificial manure,20 the extensive research of Han and his 
colleagues on making straw more digestible to cattle through use of microor- 
ganisms,21 and the possibility of enriching straw with earthworm protein 
prior to use as feedstock might justify additional work.

In a previous article, on production of earthworms as a potentially eco- 
nomical source of protein,’ a net yield of about 2 g of live E. foetida were ob- 
tained on a 24 cm2 area about 5 cm deep during 7 weeks between ages 20 and 
27 weeks. This net yield was in the form of cocoons and hatchlings. In the 
present work, from Table VII, based on a mean of 1300 mg live weight per 
adult in populations of eight worms at 20 or 25°C 10.4 g of biomass were 
produced in 80 days in an area 78 cm2, 1.5-2 cm deep. This converts to ap- 
proximately the same level of productivity as in the preceding study, but is 
based on a net yield in the form of adult biomass. E. foetida is a potentially 
rich source of protein, but whether this potential can be realized hinges on 
obtaining further insight into its specific nutrient requirements, on achieving 
conditions for optimizing the yield from a starting source of food, and on 
whether engineering can be devised for appropriate sequencing of operations 
in which cocoons or hatchlings would be introduced and adults harvested.
