Fluid-mechanical assumptions used by the pressure-loss and aeration-discharge model.
Internal passages are treated as smooth circular tubes. Tube friction is represented by Darcy

Flow is not treated with a full compressible momentum formulation. The code does update
local density from the ideal-gas relation using the local temperature and pressure state, but the
friction, minor-loss, and perforation-discharge closures are all used in their incompressible-form
reduced-order versions.
The optional upstream header loss and the unresolved internal splitter/manifold loss are repre-

Parallel aeration branches are assumed identical at a given operating point, so one representative
closed-end perforated branch is marched axially and then scaled up.
Perforations are smeared uniformly over the congured segment count rather than resolved hole
by hole. The end cap itself is not perforated.

14.2 Explicit assumptions for Sections 614
Section 6 (Internal convection correlations): heater and aeration in-tube convection are treated
as smooth, circular, one-dimensional internal ows with a laminar/turbulent branch switch at
Re= 2300. The implementation does not include explicit roughness corrections, transition
blending beyond that branch switch, or secondary-ow corrections.
Section 7 (External convection correlations): external free-convection coecients are represented
by ChurchillChu / horizontal-plate reduced-order closures evaluated at a lm state. For insulated
boundaries,T s is theexterior insulation-surface temperature; for open/vent top openings, the
same correlation family is applied to the exposed top-surface proxy.
Section 8 (Conduction and thermal resistance networks): the vessel wall is modeled as a planar
layered wall with lumped inside/outside convection resistances, while tube walls use exact cylin-
drical logarithmic resistances on an exposed-outer-area basis. Material properties and congured
h-values are treated as piecewise constants within each evaluation state.
Section 9 (Heater-side treatment): wire-to-air convection is correlation-derived, but the heater-
shell exterior coecient is a congured eective value representing unresolved ambient-side losses
around the insulated heater tube. The helical-wire obstruction is captured by a reduced-order
projected-area drag surrogate rather than by a resolved ribbed-passage CFD model.
Section 10 (Aeration-tube model): the aeration tube is solved as a one-dimensional axial march
with closed-end mass closure and segmentwise wall exchange. The released-air enthalpy and
evaporation eects are coupled into segment energy bookkeeping, but the in-tube temperature
state itself advances through the retained marching relation.
Section 11 (Warm-air release / jet-heating): direct released-air heating/cooling is represented as
an enthalpy source/sink term attached to released mass ow, not as a resolved jet-impingement
eld. This term is sucient for branch-scale energy closure but does not capture three-dimensional
local plume/recirculation structure.
Section 12 (Moisture and latent heat): hole-scale evaporation coupling uses a Frössling-Sherwood
mass-transfer closure driven by local perforation discharge velocity, with additional carrying-
capacity and available-heat limits. Reusing this reduced-order closure is reasonable at the current
model delity because it preserves the dominantRe-dependent transfer trend without adding a
full interfacial transport solve.
Section 13 (Radial plume and habitat exclusion): plume cooling and humidity evolution are post-
processing estimates in a radial coordinateς, and unsafe habitat metrics are set-based geometric
measures on the Cartesian bed cross-section. Wall-return heating is capped at nearest tube-center
osets, assuming direct aeration dominates additional thermal inuence beyond that distance.
Section 14 (Bin-wall and ambient heat loss): side, bottom, covered-top, and vent-opening contri-
butions are modeled with orientation-specichandUvalues then collapsed to mode-dependent
